### Imaging-based Spatial Transcriptomics (iST) vs Sequencing-based Spatial Transcriptomics (sST)

Spatial Transcriptomics (ST) technologies are generally classified into two primary categories: imaging-based and sequencing-based methods <d-cite key="Williams.2022"></d-cite>

Within the imaging-based category, technologies are further divided into in situ hybridization (ISH) and in situ sequencing (ISS)

Conversely, the sequencing-based category is primarily represented by in situ capturing (ISC) techniques

Imaging-based Spatial Transcriptomics (iST) identifies different mRNA species by targeting specific genes a priori using labeled complementary probes, which allows for the direct visualization of gene distribution within their native tissue context

Because of this targeted approach, imaging methods are often highly sensitive and capable of achieving subcellular or even single-molecule resolution

However, this reliance on specialized probes means that most imaging-based methods suffer from lower gene throughput and more limited gene panels than unbiased sequencing methods

Furthermore, as these techniques often require prior knowledge of the target genes to design the necessary labels, the total number of genes that can be measured simultaneously is restricted compared to transcriptome-wide approaches

Sequencing-based Spatial Transcriptomics (sST), primarily represented by in situ capturing (ISC), utilizes spatially barcoded arrays to capture polyadenylated RNA transcripts directly from tissue sections for analysis via next-generation sequencing (NGS) 

Unlike imaging-based methods, sST is unbiased and provides transcriptome-wide coverage, meaning no a priori knowledge of target genes is required; this makes it ideal for discovery-driven research and identifying novel biological mechanisms or rare transcripts

While early iterations like the standard Visium platform are limited by a resolution of 55 µm diameter spots—which typically encompass a mixture of multiple cells—newer high-definition technologies such as Visium HD have significantly advanced this resolution

Visium HD reduces the spot diameter to 2 µm and eliminates gaps between spots, enabling gap-free and bias-free profiling at single-cell scale resolution


Despite these advancements in resolution, ISC technologies generally encounter lower transcript capture efficiency compared to imaging-based methods, as they often rely on the vertical diffusion of transcripts onto a capture surface rather than direct in-tissue analysis

### What does Spatial Transcriptomics unlock compared to other transcriptomics techniques?
The following overview highlights what SRT unlocks compared to its predecessors:
1. Overcoming the Limitations of Bulk Transcriptomics
For two decades, bulk RNA-seq was the standard for characterizing biological samples
. However, its primary drawback is that it measures average genomic profiles across biological specimens containing thousands to millions of cells
.
What SRT Unlocks: SRT captures the underlying cellular heterogeneity that is masked by the dominant populations in bulk approaches
. While bulk transcriptomics disregards spatial information entirely, SRT preserves it, allowing for the identification of rare cell areas that would otherwise be lost in the "ensemble" measurement
.
2. SRT versus scRNA-seq: Context and Architecture
While scRNA-seq advanced the field to the single-cell level, it relies on the dissociation of tissue samples into a single-cell suspension
.
Preserving Physical Organization: According to Chen and Larsson (2024), scRNA-seq cannot preserve information about the physical organization of cells, which is vital to many biological processes
. SRT complements scRNA-seq by placing biological phenomena in their original physical context
.
Protecting Fragile Cell Types: Dissociation for scRNA-seq can be destructive, leading to the loss of fragile cell types or inducing stress-related artifacts in gene expression
. SRT profiles gene expression in situ, avoiding the need to isolate viable cells and thus better restoring the "real sample state"
.
Accessibility of Complex Structures: Certain cells with complex morphologies, such as neurons and glial cells in the brain, are difficult to access via tissue enzymolysis for scRNA-seq
. SRT allows these delicate structures to be studied intact
.
3. Unlocking Intercellular and Subcellular Insights
By maintaining spatial coordinates, SRT enables analyses that are mathematically impossible with dissociated data:
Cell-Cell Interactions: SRT allows researchers to identify interactions between different cell types in specific regions, such as signaling pathways and ligand-receptor pairs that act based on physical proximity
. This is essential for understanding how the tumor microenvironment (TME) influences cancer cell growth or immune evasion
.
Tissue Niches and Neighborhoods: Researchers can identify functional regions and microenvironmental niches (e.g., the tumor-normal tissue interface or tertiary lymphoid structures) defined by both gene expression patterns and physical proximity
.
Subcellular Localization: Some imaging-based SRT methods can resolve the precise position of individual RNA molecules at subcellular or even single-molecule resolution
. This reveals how the localization of mRNAs varies according to gene function—regulating, for example, where a protein product is trafficked—an insight scRNA-seq cannot provide
.
4. Enabling Spatiotemporal Atlases
SRT provides the foundation for constructing 3D and temporal transcriptomic maps
.
3D Reconstruction: By aligning consecutive 2D tissue sections, SRT allows for the reconstruction of full 3D volumes of molecular tissue anatomy
.
Developmental Tracking: It enables the dynamic investigation of development (spatiotemporal transcriptomics), allowing researchers to track cellular resolution developmental trajectories as they relate to specific spatial events in embryos or regenerating organs
.

Another thing we can do is to remove sparse spatial regions using computed connectivities. This is a good idea if we have a sample that is not as compact as the dataset we are working with, where we may have "isolated islands" of cells. This should be evaluated on a project bases, as these islands could be real and not artifacts from sample preparation. 

We can do this using the `connected_components` function from `scipy.sparse.csgraph`. This function below requires connectivities to be calculated first, which we will do in the next section with `Novae`.

{% highlight python %}
def filter_isolated_regions(
    adatas,
    min_cells=2000,
    run_dir=None,
    remove_specific_regions=None,
):
    from scipy.sparse.csgraph import (
        connected_components,
    )

    for i, adata in enumerate(adatas):
        coords = adata.obsm["spatial"]

        sample_id = adata.obs[
            "sample_id"
        ].unique()[0]

        logger.info(
            f"Running region filtering based on number of cells (removing isolated islands). {sample_id}"
        )

        print(
            f"X range: {coords[:, 0].min():.1f} - {coords[:, 0].max():.1f}"
        )
        print(
            f"Y range: {coords[:, 1].min():.1f} - {coords[:, 1].max():.1f}"
        )

        n_components, labels = (
            connected_components(
                adata.obsp[
                    "spatial_connectivities"
                ],
                directed=False,
            )
        )
        adata.obs["spatial_region"] = (
            labels.astype(str)
        )
        print(
            f"Found {n_components} spatial regions"
        )

        # Count cells per region
        region_counts = adata.obs[
            "spatial_region"
        ].value_counts()
        print(region_counts)

        large_regions = region_counts[
            region_counts >= min_cells
        ].index.tolist()
        print(
            f"Large regions (>={min_cells} cells): {large_regions}"
        )

        # Tag regions as "main" or "island"
        adata.obs["region_type"] = (
            adata.obs[
                "spatial_region"
            ].apply(
                lambda x: "main"
                if x in large_regions
                else "island"
            )
        )

        # Visualize the regions
        fig, axes = plt.subplots(
            1, 3, figsize=(14, 5)
        )

        # Plot all regions colored
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="spatial_region",
            ax=axes[0],
            show=False,
            title="All spatial regions (based on connectivities)",
        )

        # Plot main vs island
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="region_type",
            ax=axes[1],
            show=False,
            title="Main vs Islands",
        )

        if (
            remove_specific_regions
            is not None
        ):
            if (
                sample_id
                in remove_specific_regions
            ):
                regions_to_remove = remove_specific_regions[
                    sample_id
                ]
                adata = adata[
                    ~adata.obs[
                        "spatial_region"
                    ].isin(
                        regions_to_remove
                    )
                ]

                logger.info(
                    f"Removed user defined regions from {sample_id}: {regions_to_remove}"
                )
        cells_before_filtering = (
            adata.n_obs
        )
        # Filter to keep only main regions
        adata = adata[
            adata.obs["region_type"]
            == "main"
        ]
        cells_after_filtering = (
            adata.n_obs
        )
        print(
            f"Filtered: {cells_before_filtering} -> {cells_after_filtering} cells"
        )
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="region_type",
            ax=axes[2],
            show=False,
            title="After filtering isolated islands",
        )
        plt.tight_layout()

        if run_dir is not None:
            plt.savefig(
                f"{run_dir}/spatial_regions_{sample_id}.png",
                dpi=300,
                bbox_inches="tight",
            )
        else:
            plt.savefig(
                f"spatial_regions_{sample_id}.png",
                dpi=300,
                bbox_inches="tight",
            )
        plt.show()

        adatas[i] = adata.copy()

    return adatas

## Cell Type Annotation using Enrichmap and Cell Type Gene Set Signatures

For cell type annotation, one can generally used methods that require a reference dataset, such as the new implementation of the RCTD method in python <d-cite key="cable2022robust"></d-cite> (check their [repository](https://github.com/p-gueguen/rctd-py)), or gene set signatures from cell types that can be fetched from databases such as PanglaoDB, CellMarker 2.0 or trained models such as CellTypist and literature. For some diseases, it may be easier to find scRNA-seq datasets that may be used to perform cell type annotation, however, in rarer cancer types such as SCLC, this may not exist. Therefore, the best approach is to use cell type gene signatures and calculate scores to then annotate the segemnted cells and identified domains/niches/clusters.

We will now use the `Enrichmap` package to perform cell type annotation <d-cite key="Celik.2025"></d-cite>. (Talk more about this package).

We can then use these scores together with a elbow-gap method to assign cell types to each cell ID based on the compured scores. The reason why we can try this approach is because a given cell ID might still be capturing multiple cells (cell segmentation is working well, but is not perfect). If the scores are close to each other (e.g. the meanchanges from the ranking dataframe above) for a given domain, then we can assume that the cell ID is capturing multiple cells and we should assign it to the most enriched cell type(s).

{% highlight python linenos %}
enrichmap_gap_annotations = {}
elbow_data = {}

for domain in ranking_df[
    "group"
].unique():
    domain_data = ranking_df.loc[
        ranking_df["group"] == domain
    ].copy()

    # Filter to significant, positively enriched
    domain_data = domain_data.loc[
        (domain_data["meanchange"] > 0)
        & (domain_data["padj"] < 0.05)
    ]

    if len(domain_data) == 0:
        enrichmap_gap_annotations[
            domain
        ] = "Unknown"
        continue

    if len(domain_data) == 1:
        enrichmap_gap_annotations[
            domain
        ] = domain_data["name"].iloc[0]
        elbow_data[domain] = domain_data
        continue

    elbow_data[domain] = domain_data

    sorted_mc = domain_data[
        "meanchange"
    ].values
    gaps = (
        sorted_mc[:-1] - sorted_mc[1:]
    )
    biggest_gap_idx = int(
        np.argmax(gaps)
    )

    selected = (
        domain_data["name"]
        .iloc[: biggest_gap_idx + 1]
        .tolist()
    )
    enrichmap_gap_annotations[
        domain
    ] = "/".join(selected)

logger.info(
    f"Domain -> cell type mapping:\n{enrichmap_gap_annotations}"
)

n_cols_plot = min(4, n_domains_plot)
n_rows_plot = int(
    np.ceil(
        n_domains_plot / n_cols_plot
    )
)

fig_elbow, axes_elbow = plt.subplots(
    n_rows_plot,
    n_cols_plot,
    figsize=(
        5 * n_cols_plot,
        4 * n_rows_plot,
    ),
)
axes_flat = np.array(
    axes_elbow
).flatten()

for ax_idx, domain in enumerate(
    plottable_domains
):
    ax = axes_flat[ax_idx]
    domain_data = elbow_data[domain]
    mc_vals = domain_data[
        "meanchange"
    ].values
    ct_names = domain_data[
        "name"
    ].values
    x_pos = np.arange(len(mc_vals))

    selected_names = set(
        enrichmap_gap_annotations[
            domain
        ].split("/")
    )

    ax.plot(
        x_pos,
        mc_vals,
        color="gray",
        lw=1.5,
        zorder=2,
    )

    for i, (xp, mc, name) in enumerate(
        zip(x_pos, mc_vals, ct_names)
    ):
        if name in selected_names:
            color = "steelblue"
            ec = "black"
        else:
            color = "silver"
            ec = "gray"
        ax.scatter(
            xp,
            mc,
            color=color,
            s=60,
            zorder=4,
            edgecolors=ec,
            linewidths=0.5,
        )

    if len(mc_vals) > 1:
        gaps = (
            mc_vals[:-1] - mc_vals[1:]
        )
        gap_idx = int(np.argmax(gaps))
        gap_x = gap_idx + 0.5
        gap_val = gaps[gap_idx]
        ax.axvline(
            gap_x,
            color="red",
            ls="--",
            lw=1,
            alpha=0.8,
            label=f"gap = {gap_val:.3f}",
        )
        ax.axvspan(
            -0.5,
            gap_x,
            alpha=0.08,
            color="steelblue",
        )
        y_top = mc_vals[gap_idx]
        y_bot = mc_vals[gap_idx + 1]
        bracket_x = gap_x + 0.3
        ax.annotate(
            "",
            xy=(bracket_x, y_bot),
            xytext=(bracket_x, y_top),
            arrowprops=dict(
                arrowstyle="<->",
                color="red",
                lw=1.2,
            ),
        )
        ax.text(
            bracket_x + 0.15,
            (y_top + y_bot) / 2,
            f"{gap_val:.3f}",
            fontsize=6,
            color="red",
            va="center",
        )

    ax.set_xticks(x_pos)
    ax.set_xticklabels(
        ct_names,
        rotation=60,
        ha="right",
        fontsize=5,
    )
    ax.set_ylabel(
        "meanchange", fontsize=7
    )
    ax.set_title(
        f"Domain {domain}", fontsize=8
    )
    ax.legend(
        fontsize=5, loc="upper right"
    )
    ax.tick_params(labelsize=6)

for ax_idx in range(
    n_domains_plot, len(axes_flat)
):
    axes_flat[ax_idx].axis("off")

fig_elbow.suptitle(
