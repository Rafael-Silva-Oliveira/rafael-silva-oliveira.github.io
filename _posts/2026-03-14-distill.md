## Purpose of this tutorial

Spatial transcriptomics is transforming our understanding of tissue biology by combining gene expression profiling with spatial context <d-cite key="Williams.2022"></d-cite> <d-cite key="Moses.2022"></d-cite>. However, working with high-resolution platforms like Visium HD still requires navigating a complex pipeline: from reading and segmenting cells, to identifying spatial domains, and finally annotating cell types.

This tutorial provides a practical, end-to-end walkthrough of how to process a Visium HD dataset using a Python-based pipeline. Specifically, we will cover:

1. **Cell segmentation** using [SOPA](https://github.com/gustaveroussy/sopa) <d-cite key="Blampey.2024"></d-cite>, combining StarDist <d-cite key="Schmidt.2018"></d-cite> for initial nuclear segmentation with ProSeg <d-cite key="Jones.2024b"></d-cite> for transcript-informed boundary refinement.
2. **Spatial domain identification** using [Novae](https://github.com/MICS-Lab/novae) <d-cite key="Blampey.2024b"></d-cite>, a graph-based foundation model that learns cell representations from their spatial context and natively handles batch correction.
3. **Cell type annotation** using [Enrichmap](https://github.com/cenkcelik/enrichmap) <d-cite key="Celik.2025"></d-cite>, a spatially-aware enrichment scoring method with built-in batch correction and spatial smoothing, combined with gene set signatures to assign cell types to segmented cells and spatial domains.

We use a publicly available Human Colorectal Cancer (CRC) FFPE dataset from [10x Genomics](https://www.10xgenomics.com/datasets/visium-hd-cytassist-gene-expression-libraries-of-human-crc) and reproduce the key findings from their Loupe Browser tutorial, but entirely in Python and at single-cell resolution through cell segmentation. By the end, you will have a fully annotated spatial transcriptomics dataset ready for downstream biological analyses.

## Spatial Transcriptomics: A primer

Understanding tissue function requires knowing not just *which* genes a cell expresses, but *where* that cell sits relative to its neighbors. Spatial transcriptomics (ST) has emerged over the past decade as a family of technologies that capture spatially resolved gene expression, bridging the gap between traditional histology and molecular profiling <d-cite key="Moses.2022"></d-cite> <d-cite key="Cheng.2023"></d-cite>.

The field has advanced rapidly: from early methods that profiled a handful of genes to modern platforms capable of transcriptome-wide measurements at near-single-cell resolution <d-cite key="Yue.2023"></d-cite>. These technologies have found broad applications in cancer biology <d-cite key="Chen.2024j"></d-cite> <d-cite key="Park.2023"></d-cite>, neuroscience, developmental biology, and drug discovery <d-cite key="Cao.2024b"></d-cite>. In this section, we provide a brief primer on the two main families of ST technologies and what they unlock compared to earlier approaches.

### Imaging-based Spatial Transcriptomics (iST) vs Sequencing-based Spatial Transcriptomics (sST)

Spatial transcriptomics (ST) technologies are generally classified into two primary categories: **imaging-based** and **sequencing-based** methods <d-cite key="Williams.2022"></d-cite> <d-cite key="Cheng.2023"></d-cite>.

Within the imaging-based category, technologies are further divided into **in situ hybridization** (ISH) and **in situ sequencing** (ISS). Conversely, the sequencing-based category is primarily represented by **in situ capturing** (ISC) techniques.

**Imaging-based Spatial Transcriptomics (iST)** identifies different mRNA species by targeting specific genes *a priori* using labeled complementary probes, which allows for the direct visualization of gene distribution within their native tissue context <d-cite key="Moses.2022"></d-cite>. Because of this targeted approach, imaging methods are often highly sensitive and capable of achieving subcellular or even single-molecule resolution. Commercial platforms in this category include MERFISH (Vizgen's MERSCOPE), smFISH-based methods (10x Genomics' Xenium), and CosMx (NanoString). However, this reliance on specialized probes means that most imaging-based methods suffer from lower gene throughput and more limited gene panels than unbiased sequencing methods. Furthermore, as these techniques require prior knowledge of the target genes to design the necessary labels, the total number of genes that can be measured simultaneously is restricted compared to transcriptome-wide approaches <d-cite key="Yue.2023"></d-cite>.

**Sequencing-based Spatial Transcriptomics (sST)**, primarily represented by in situ capturing (ISC), utilizes spatially barcoded arrays to capture polyadenylated RNA transcripts directly from tissue sections for analysis via next-generation sequencing (NGS) <d-cite key="Williams.2022"></d-cite>. Unlike imaging-based methods, sST is unbiased and provides transcriptome-wide coverage, meaning no *a priori* knowledge of target genes is required; this makes it ideal for discovery-driven research and identifying novel biological mechanisms or rare transcripts.

While early iterations like the standard Visium platform are limited by a resolution of 55 µm diameter spots -- which typically encompass a mixture of multiple cells -- newer high-definition technologies such as Visium HD have significantly advanced this resolution. Visium HD reduces the spot diameter to 2 µm and eliminates gaps between spots, enabling gap-free and bias-free profiling at single-cell scale resolution.

Despite these advancements in resolution, ISC technologies generally encounter lower transcript capture efficiency compared to imaging-based methods, as they often rely on the vertical diffusion of transcripts onto a capture surface rather than direct in-tissue analysis <d-cite key="Cheng.2023"></d-cite>.

### What does Spatial Transcriptomics unlock compared to other transcriptomics techniques?
The following overview highlights what SRT unlocks compared to its predecessors:

**1. Overcoming the Limitations of Bulk Transcriptomics.** For two decades, bulk RNA-seq was the standard for characterizing biological samples. However, its primary drawback is that it measures average genomic profiles across biological specimens containing thousands to millions of cells <d-cite key="Cao.2024b"></d-cite>. SRT captures the underlying cellular heterogeneity that is masked by the dominant populations in bulk approaches. While bulk transcriptomics disregards spatial information entirely, SRT preserves it, allowing for the identification of rare cell populations that would otherwise be lost in the "ensemble" measurement.

**2. SRT versus scRNA-seq: Context and Architecture.** While scRNA-seq advanced the field to the single-cell level, it relies on the dissociation of tissue samples into a single-cell suspension.
- **Preserving Physical Organization:** scRNA-seq cannot preserve information about the physical organization of cells, which is vital to many biological processes <d-cite key="Chen.2024j"></d-cite>. SRT complements scRNA-seq by placing biological phenomena in their original physical context.
- **Protecting Fragile Cell Types:** Dissociation for scRNA-seq can be destructive, leading to the loss of fragile cell types or inducing stress-related artifacts in gene expression <d-cite key="Williams.2022"></d-cite>. SRT profiles gene expression *in situ*, avoiding the need to isolate viable cells and thus better preserving the true sample state.
- **Accessibility of Complex Structures:** Certain cells with complex morphologies, such as neurons and glial cells in the brain, are difficult to access via tissue enzymolysis for scRNA-seq <d-cite key="Park.2023"></d-cite>. SRT allows these delicate structures to be studied intact.

**3. Unlocking Intercellular and Subcellular Insights.** By maintaining spatial coordinates, SRT enables analyses that are impossible with dissociated data:
- **Cell-Cell Interactions:** SRT allows researchers to identify interactions between different cell types in specific regions, such as signaling pathways and ligand-receptor pairs that act based on physical proximity <d-cite key="Chen.2024j"></d-cite>. This is essential for understanding how the tumor microenvironment (TME) influences cancer cell growth or immune evasion.
- **Tissue Niches and Neighborhoods:** Researchers can identify functional regions and microenvironmental niches (e.g., the tumor-normal tissue interface or tertiary lymphoid structures) defined by both gene expression patterns and physical proximity.
- **Subcellular Localization:** Some imaging-based SRT methods can resolve the precise position of individual RNA molecules at subcellular or even single-molecule resolution <d-cite key="Cheng.2023"></d-cite>. This reveals how the localization of mRNAs varies according to gene function -- regulating, for example, where a protein product is trafficked -- an insight scRNA-seq cannot provide.

**4. Enabling Spatiotemporal Atlases.** SRT provides the foundation for constructing 3D and temporal transcriptomic maps <d-cite key="Khan.2025"></d-cite>.
- **3D Reconstruction:** By aligning consecutive 2D tissue sections, SRT allows for the reconstruction of full 3D volumes of molecular tissue anatomy.
- **Developmental Tracking:** It enables the dynamic investigation of development (spatiotemporal transcriptomics), allowing researchers to track cellular resolution developmental trajectories as they relate to specific spatial events in embryos or regenerating organs.

### Shift from Deconvolution (spot based) to Cell Segmentation (bin based)

Early sequencing-based ST platforms like the original Visium captured gene expression at 55 µm spots, each typically containing a mixture of 1-10 cells. To infer the cell type composition within each spot, computational **deconvolution** methods such as RCTD <d-cite key="cable2022robust"></d-cite> were developed. These approaches use a scRNA-seq reference to estimate the proportion of each cell type contributing to a spot's expression profile.

With the advent of Visium HD and its 2 µm bin resolution, the paradigm has shifted. Instead of deconvolving mixed spots, we can now perform **cell segmentation** directly on the high-resolution histology image and assign transcripts to individual cells. This approach produces a per-cell gene expression matrix that is conceptually similar to scRNA-seq, but with spatial coordinates preserved. The result is a more direct and interpretable representation of the tissue, where each observation corresponds to a single segmented cell rather than a statistical mixture.

{% include figure.liquid loading="eager" path="assets/img/segmentation_and_annotation/Screenshot 2026-03-18 135813.png" class="img-fluid rounded z-depth-1" %}

This shift from deconvolution to segmentation is a key motivation for this tutorial: by segmenting cells on Visium HD data, we obtain single-cell resolution spatial transcriptomics without the assumptions and limitations inherent to deconvolution-based approaches.

## Cell Segmentation in Single‑Cell Spatial Transcriptomics using SOPA

Now that we have the data loaded and cropped, we can start segmenting the cells. We will use the SOPA (Spatial Omics Pipeline and Analysis) package <d-cite key="Blampey.2024"></d-cite> to do this. SOPA is a technology-invariant, memory-efficient pipeline built on top of the [SpatialData](https://spatialdata.scverse.org/) framework that unifies analysis across all image-based spatial omics platforms (Xenium, MERSCOPE, CosMx, Visium HD, and others). You can check it out on [GitHub](https://github.com/gustaveroussy/sopa).

SOPA handles the full pipeline from reading raw data to segmentation, transcript aggregation, and annotation. For segmentation specifically, it provides a patch-based approach that divides the tissue image into manageable patches, runs segmentation on each, and then stitches the results back together -- making it feasible to process large whole-slide images without running out of memory.

### The landscape of cell segmentation methods

Cell segmentation is a critical step in spatial transcriptomics, as the accuracy of all downstream analyses (domain identification, cell type annotation, cell-cell interaction analysis) depends on correctly delineating cell boundaries and assigning transcripts to cells. The landscape of transcript-informed segmentation methods can be broadly organized into three methodological families:

**CNNs and Transformers.** These methods leverage deep learning architectures trained on microscopy images. They include BIDCell (transcripts only, no priors required), Bin2Cell (transcripts only), SCS and UCS (which use transcripts combined with nuclear/cell images and cell/nuclear masks as priors), Bering (transcripts + images, no priors), and GeneSegNet (transcripts + images, no priors). StarDist <d-cite key="Schmidt.2018"></d-cite>, while not shown in the figure above, is one of the most widely used methods in this category -- it detects cell nuclei via star-convex polygons using a CNN and is the default segmentation method in SOPA.

**Graph-based methods.** Segger and ComSeg model transcript-to-cell assignment as a graph problem. Segger uses transcripts combined with nuclear/cell images and cell/nuclear mask priors, while ComSeg operates on transcripts only using scRNA-seq references. These approaches can capture complex cell geometries that are difficult to represent with simpler shape models.

**Probabilistic and Bayesian methods.** ProSeg <d-cite key="Jones.2024b"></d-cite> and Baysor take a generative modeling approach, simulating plausible cell boundaries from transcript distributions. ProSeg uses nuclear/cell masks as priors and operates in 3D (accounting for x, y, z coordinates of transcripts), while Baysor can optionally use scRNA-seq references and also supports 3D.

The choice of segmentation method depends on the available inputs (H&E/DAPI images, nuclear staining, transcript coordinates), the desired resolution, and computational constraints. In this tutorial, we use a two-step strategy: **StarDist** for initial nuclear segmentation from the H&E image, followed by **ProSeg** for transcript-informed boundary refinement. This combination leverages the strengths of both approaches -- StarDist provides robust nuclear detection, while ProSeg refines boundaries using the actual transcript spatial distribution.

### StarDist

StarDist <d-cite key="Schmidt.2018"></d-cite> is a deep learning method for cell/nucleus detection that represents objects as **star-convex polygons**. For every pixel in the image, the model predicts (1) the probability that the pixel belongs to a cell nucleus, and (2) the distances from that pixel to the boundary of the object along a set of predefined radial directions. This star-convex representation is a much better shape approximation for rounded nuclei than axis-aligned bounding boxes, and it avoids the need for a separate shape refinement step. StarDist is particularly well-suited for crowded cell scenarios where traditional segmentation approaches often falsely merge neighboring cells or miss valid instances.

### Proseg

ProSeg <d-cite key="Jones.2024b"></d-cite> (Probabilistic Segmentation) takes a fundamentally different approach to cell segmentation by adopting methods from *ab initio* cell simulation. Rather than detecting cell boundaries from images alone, ProSeg infers morphologically plausible cell boundaries directly from the spatial distribution of transcripts. It models cell shapes as probabilistic distributions and iteratively refines boundaries to maximize the likelihood of the observed transcript positions, while preserving cell type heterogeneity.

A key advantage of ProSeg is that it can use prior segmentation results (e.g., from StarDist) as initial boundary estimates and then refine them using transcript information. This is exactly what we do in this tutorial: StarDist provides the initial nuclear boundaries from the H&E image, and ProSeg refines these boundaries to better capture the true extent of each cell based on where its transcripts are located. ProSeg has been benchmarked across datasets from three commercial platforms and shows superior performance and computational efficiency compared to existing methods. Notably, improved segmentation accuracy with ProSeg aids in the detection of difficult-to-segment cell types such as tumor-infiltrating immune cells (e.g., neutrophils and T cells).

{% details Click here to see an optional/aditional step to remove "isolated cell islands" using connectivities in sparser samples %}
Another thing we can do is to remove sparse spatial regions using computed connectivities. This is a good idea if we have a sample that is not as compact as the dataset we are working with, where we may have "isolated islands" of cells. This should be evaluated on a project bases, as these islands could be real and not artifacts from sample preparation. 

We can do this using the `connected_components` function from `scipy.sparse.csgraph`. This function below requires connectivities to be calculated first, which we will do in the next section with `Novae`.

{% highlight python %}
def filter_isolated_regions(
    adatas,
    min_cells=2000,
    run_dir=None,
    remove_specific_regions=None,
):
    from scipy.sparse.csgraph import (
        connected_components,
    )

    for i, adata in enumerate(adatas):
        coords = adata.obsm["spatial"]

        sample_id = adata.obs[
            "sample_id"
        ].unique()[0]

        logger.info(
            f"Running region filtering based on number of cells (removing isolated islands). {sample_id}"
        )

        print(
            f"X range: {coords[:, 0].min():.1f} - {coords[:, 0].max():.1f}"
        )
        print(
            f"Y range: {coords[:, 1].min():.1f} - {coords[:, 1].max():.1f}"
        )

        n_components, labels = (
            connected_components(
                adata.obsp[
                    "spatial_connectivities"
                ],
                directed=False,
            )
        )
        adata.obs["spatial_region"] = (
            labels.astype(str)
        )
        print(
            f"Found {n_components} spatial regions"
        )

        # Count cells per region
        region_counts = adata.obs[
            "spatial_region"
        ].value_counts()
        print(region_counts)

        large_regions = region_counts[
            region_counts >= min_cells
        ].index.tolist()
        print(
            f"Large regions (>={min_cells} cells): {large_regions}"
        )

        # Tag regions as "main" or "island"
        adata.obs["region_type"] = (
            adata.obs[
                "spatial_region"
            ].apply(
                lambda x: "main"
                if x in large_regions
                else "island"
            )
        )

        # Visualize the regions
        fig, axes = plt.subplots(
            1, 3, figsize=(14, 5)
        )

        # Plot all regions colored
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="spatial_region",
            ax=axes[0],
            show=False,
            title="All spatial regions (based on connectivities)",
        )

        # Plot main vs island
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="region_type",
            ax=axes[1],
            show=False,
            title="Main vs Islands",
        )

        if (
            remove_specific_regions
            is not None
        ):
            if (
                sample_id
                in remove_specific_regions
            ):
                regions_to_remove = remove_specific_regions[
                    sample_id
                ]
                adata = adata[
                    ~adata.obs[
                        "spatial_region"
                    ].isin(
                        regions_to_remove
                    )
                ]

                logger.info(
                    f"Removed user defined regions from {sample_id}: {regions_to_remove}"
                )
        cells_before_filtering = (
            adata.n_obs
        )
        # Filter to keep only main regions
        adata = adata[
            adata.obs["region_type"]
            == "main"
        ]
        cells_after_filtering = (
            adata.n_obs
        )
        print(
            f"Filtered: {cells_before_filtering} -> {cells_after_filtering} cells"
        )
        sc.pl.embedding(
            adata,
            basis="spatial",
            color="region_type",
            ax=axes[2],
            show=False,
            title="After filtering isolated islands",
        )
        plt.tight_layout()

        if run_dir is not None:
            plt.savefig(
                f"{run_dir}/spatial_regions_{sample_id}.png",
                dpi=300,
                bbox_inches="tight",
            )
        else:
            plt.savefig(
                f"spatial_regions_{sample_id}.png",
                dpi=300,
                bbox_inches="tight",
            )
        plt.show()

        adatas[i] = adata.copy()

    return adatas

## Spatial Clustering and Domain Identification with Novae

Once we have segmented cells and pre-processed the data, the next step is to identify **spatial domains** -- regions of the tissue that share similar gene expression patterns and spatial organization. These domains correspond to biologically meaningful structures such as tumor regions, stroma, immune infiltrates, or normal mucosa.

We will use [Novae](https://github.com/MICS-Lab/novae) <d-cite key="Blampey.2024b"></d-cite>, a graph-based foundation model for spatial transcriptomics. Novae was pre-trained on nearly 30 million cells across 18 tissues, enabling it to perform zero-shot domain inference across different gene panels, tissues, and technologies. Key advantages of Novae include:

- **Built-in batch correction:** Novae natively corrects batch effects during representation learning, which is critical when working with multiple samples or slides.
- **Hierarchical domain structure:** It constructs a nested hierarchy of spatial domains, allowing you to explore tissue organization at different resolutions.
- **Foundation model approach:** Because it was pre-trained on a large and diverse corpus of spatial transcriptomics data, Novae can generalize to new datasets without extensive fine-tuning.

An alternative to Novae for spatial clustering is [SpatialLeiden](https://github.com/HiDiHlabs/SpatialLeiden), a spatially-aware extension of the Leiden community detection algorithm. SpatialLeiden uses a multiplex graph that combines a gene expression layer with a spatial topology layer, and a `layer_ratio` parameter controls the balance between expression similarity and spatial proximity. It integrates with the scverse ecosystem and works well with Visium, Stereo-seq, and imaging-based platforms. For this tutorial, however, we use Novae due to its foundation model capabilities and hierarchical domain structure.

One of the main steps in Novae is to compute the spatial neighbors. This is done using the `spatial_neighbors` function, which requires the `radius` parameter -- the radius of the neighborhood to be considered. For this particular cropped region, we will use a radius of 50.

Note that neighbors/connectivities need to be computed for each sample individually. If you have multiple samples in a single `AnnData` object, you can use the `slide_key` parameter to compute connectivities for each sample separately.

## Cell Type Annotation using Enrichmap and Cell Type Gene Set Signatures

For cell type annotation, one can generally use methods that require a reference dataset, such as the Python implementation of the RCTD method <d-cite key="cable2022robust"></d-cite> (check their [repository](https://github.com/p-gueguen/rctd-py)), or gene set signatures from cell types fetched from databases such as [PanglaoDB](https://panglaodb.se/), [CellMarker 2.0](http://bio-bigdata.hrbmu.edu.cn/CellMarker/), or trained models such as [CellTypist](https://www.celltypist.org/) and literature. For some diseases, it may be easier to find scRNA-seq reference datasets for cell type annotation; however, for rarer cancer types such as SCLC, suitable references may not exist. In such cases, the best approach is to use cell type gene signatures and calculate enrichment scores to annotate the segmented cells and identified domains/niches/clusters.

We will now use [Enrichmap](https://github.com/cenkcelik/enrichmap) <d-cite key="Celik.2025"></d-cite> to perform cell type annotation. Enrichmap is a Python package specifically designed to compute gene set enrichment scores in spatial transcriptomics data. What sets Enrichmap apart from standard scoring methods (e.g., `scanpy.tl.score_genes`) is its integration of three key components:

- **Batch correction:** Enrichmap corrects for batch effects on the fly, making it suitable for multi-sample and multi-slide analyses without requiring a separate batch correction step.
- **Spatial smoothing:** It applies spatial smoothing to enrichment scores, leveraging the spatial structure of the data to produce more spatially coherent scores that capture both local and global spatial patterns.
- **Spatial confounder adjustment:** It adjusts for spatial confounders that could otherwise bias the enrichment scores, resulting in more biologically meaningful representations.

Enrichmap has been benchmarked against established scoring techniques using spatial autocorrelation metrics (e.g., Moran's I) and shows improved spatial coherence compared to conventional approaches. Importantly, it is platform-agnostic and has been demonstrated across Visium, Xenium, MERFISH, and imaging mass cytometry data.

We will start by filtering our marker genes from the `yaml` file to those present in our dataset, and then run `em.tl.score` to perform the enrichment analysis and generate per-cell scores for each cell type signature.

We can then use these scores together with an elbow-gap method to assign cell types to each cell ID based on the computed scores. The rationale behind this approach is that a given cell ID might still be capturing multiple cells (cell segmentation works well but is not perfect). If the scores are close to each other (e.g., the meanchanges from the ranking dataframe above) for a given domain, then we can infer that the domain may contain a mixture of cell types and assign it to the most enriched cell type(s).

{% highlight python linenos %}
enrichmap_gap_annotations = {}
elbow_data = {}

for domain in ranking_df[
    "group"
].unique():
    domain_data = ranking_df.loc[
        ranking_df["group"] == domain
    ].copy()

    # Filter to significant, positively enriched
    domain_data = domain_data.loc[
        (domain_data["meanchange"] > 0)
        & (domain_data["padj"] < 0.05)
    ]

    if len(domain_data) == 0:
        enrichmap_gap_annotations[
            domain
        ] = "Unknown"
        continue

    if len(domain_data) == 1:
        enrichmap_gap_annotations[
            domain
        ] = domain_data["name"].iloc[0]
        elbow_data[domain] = domain_data
        continue

    elbow_data[domain] = domain_data

    sorted_mc = domain_data[
        "meanchange"
    ].values
    gaps = (
        sorted_mc[:-1] - sorted_mc[1:]
    )
    biggest_gap_idx = int(
        np.argmax(gaps)
    )

    selected = (
        domain_data["name"]
        .iloc[: biggest_gap_idx + 1]
        .tolist()
    )
    enrichmap_gap_annotations[
        domain
    ] = "/".join(selected)

logger.info(
    f"Domain -> cell type mapping:\n{enrichmap_gap_annotations}"
)

# Assign domain-level cell type
adata.obs["cell_type_domain"] = (
    adata.obs[clustering_col].map(
        enrichmap_gap_annotations
    )
)

# Per-cell high granularity: argmax over scores
score_df = adata.obs[score_cols].copy()
score_df.columns = [
    col.replace("_score", "")
    for col in score_cols
]

## Conclusion and Next Steps

In this tutorial, we walked through a complete end-to-end pipeline for processing Visium HD spatial transcriptomics data in Python. Starting from the raw SpaceRanger outputs, we:

1. **Segmented cells** using a two-step approach with StarDist (nuclear detection) and ProSeg (transcript-informed boundary refinement) through the SOPA pipeline <d-cite key="Blampey.2024"></d-cite>.
2. **Identified spatial domains** using Novae <d-cite key="Blampey.2024b"></d-cite>, a graph-based foundation model that learns spatially-aware cell representations with built-in batch correction.
3. **Annotated cell types** using Enrichmap <d-cite key="Celik.2025"></d-cite> enrichment scores combined with an elbow-gap method to assign cell types at both the domain and single-cell level.

We were able to reproduce the key findings from the 10x Genomics Loupe Browser CRC tutorial, but entirely in Python and at single-cell resolution through cell segmentation. This approach offers greater flexibility for custom analyses and integrates seamlessly with the broader scverse ecosystem (Scanpy, SpatialData, AnnData).

### Next Steps

With a fully segmented and annotated spatial transcriptomics dataset in hand, several powerful downstream analyses become possible:

- **Cell-cell communication and ligand-receptor analysis.** Tools such as [LIANA+](https://liana-py.readthedocs.io/en/latest/) enable the inference of intercellular signaling by combining ligand-receptor databases with the spatial proximity information preserved in our data. This can reveal which cell types are communicating, through which pathways, and in which tissue regions.

- **Pathway and transcription factor activity inference.** [decoupler](https://github.com/scverse/decoupler) can be used to estimate pathway activities and transcription factor regulon scores from the gene expression data, providing functional insights beyond individual gene markers.

- **Spatially variable gene analysis.** Identifying genes whose expression varies significantly across the tissue can highlight region-specific biology. Both Novae and tools like SpatialDE or Squidpy provide methods for detecting spatially variable genes.

- **Multi-sample integration and 3D reconstruction.** For studies with multiple tissue sections, methods for spatial alignment and integration <d-cite key="Khan.2025"></d-cite> can be used to build cohesive multi-slide or even 3D reconstructions of the tissue.

- **Niche and microenvironment characterization.** Going beyond domain identification, one can characterize the cellular composition and functional state of specific niches (e.g., tumor-stroma interface, immune-enriched regions) to understand how the microenvironment shapes disease progression.

- **Clinical and translational applications.** Spatial transcriptomics is increasingly being used to discover prognostic biomarkers, understand drug resistance mechanisms, and guide therapeutic strategies <d-cite key="Chen.2024j"></d-cite> <d-cite key="Cao.2024b"></d-cite>. The pipeline presented here provides a foundation for such translational studies.

The field of spatial transcriptomics is evolving rapidly, and the tools showcased in this tutorial -- SOPA, Novae, and Enrichmap -- are under active development. We encourage readers to check their respective documentation pages for the latest features and best practices.
